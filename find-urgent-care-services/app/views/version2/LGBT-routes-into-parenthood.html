LGBT+ ways to become a parent

There are several ways you can become a parent if you are LGBT+, including:

  • IUI (intrauterine insemination)
  • IVF (in vitro fertilisation)
  • surrogacy
  • adoption or fostering

IUI and IVF can sometimes be done on the NHS. This depends on things like age and where you live. Check with a GP or local CCG (clinical commissioning group) to find out what's available in your area.

Surrogacy is not available on the NHS.

All these options can be used by anyone, including single people, same sex couples and people with fertility problems.

IUI (intrauterine insemination)

IUI (intrauterine insemination) is also called artificial insemination, or donor insemination.

Sperm is put in the womb of the person getting pregnant. This can be done at home or a licensed fertility clinic. The sperm can be from someone you know or from a sperm bank.

Information:

In the UK, the HFEA makes sure licensed fertility clinics run safely and legally.

Find a licensed clinic with HFEA's fertility clinic search

If you go to a licensed fertility clinic, they will check the sperm for sexually transmitted infections and inherited conditions.

Find out more about home insemination with donor sperm (HFEA).

IVF (in vitro fertilisation)

IVF is when an egg is removed from the ovaries and fertilised with sperm in a lab. The fertilised egg is then put back in the womb.

IVF can use eggs and sperm from donors, or from the people who want to become parents.

Find out more about using donated egg, sperm or embryos in treatment on the HFEA website.

Find a licensed clinic with HFEA's fertility clinic search.

Co-parenting

This is when 2 or more people team up to conceive and parent children together.

As a co-parent, you will not have sole custody of the child. It is advisable to get legal advice at an early stage of your planning.

There are many details to think about, such as how financial costs will be split.

Find out more about co-parenting on the Stonewall website.

Adoption or fostering

You can apply to adopt or foster through a local authority, or an adoption or foster agency. You do not have to live in the local authority you apply to, and you can be single.

You will have to complete an assessment before adopting or fostering, with the help of a social worker and preparation training.

Find out more:

  • New Family Social: charity for LGBT+ adoptive and foster parents

    • www.newfamilysocial.org.uk

  • GOV.UK: child adoption

    • www.gov.uk

  • GOV.UK: becoming a foster parent

    • www.gov.uk

Surrogacy

Surrogacy is when someone has a baby for people who cannot, or choose not to, get pregnant themselves.

The eggs and sperm can come from donors or from the intended parents. The surrogate can donate their own eggs.

Surrogacy is legal in the UK but it is illegal to advertise for surrogates, and the surrogate cannot be paid a fee.

Find out more:

  • GOV.UK having a child through surrogacy

    • www.gov.uk

  • GOV.UK legal rights of parents and surrogates

    • www.gov.uk

Who can be the legal parents

Up to 2 people can be named as legal parents on a child’s birth certificate.

The person who gives birth is always the child’s legal parent and named on the birth certificate.

Who the child's other legal parent can be depends on things like:

  • where and how the baby was conceived (for example, at a clinic or at home, with insemination or by having sex)
  • if you used a sperm donor, and whether the sperm was from a sperm bank or from someone you know
  • if the person who gives birth is married or in a civil partnership
  • if you used a surrogate

Parental responsibility

Having parental responsibility is not the same as being a legal parent.

More than 2 people can have parental responsibility for a child. This means you have a say in important decisions about the child's life, such as where they live or medical treatment.
