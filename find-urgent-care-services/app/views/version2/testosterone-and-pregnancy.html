Testosterone and pregnancy

Testosterone therapy is a type of hormone therapy used by people who were assigned female at birth who want to change how their body looks and works.

Testosterone can reduce breast tissue, stop periods, and make hair grow on your face.

Testosterone and fertility

Important: Testosterone and contraception

Taking testosterone can often stop you getting pregnant, but not all the time. You can still getpregnant while taking testosterone.

You need to use contraception if you do not want to get pregnant.

If you are taking testosterone and want to get pregnant, talk to your doctor

They can offer advice and may suggest you stop taking testosterone as this should make it more likely you will get pregnant.

If the idea of stopping your testosterone therapy is very distressing, there are other possibilities you could think about such as adoption or using donated eggs.

Non-urgent advice: See a GP if:

  • you are taking testosterone and you think you are pregnant

Do not stop taking testosterone before talking with your GP.

If you stop taking testosterone because you want to get pregnant you will probably start to have periods again. You may also notice changes in your body shape around your hips, chest and thighs.

Your tone of voice and facial hair should be unaffected.

Pregnancy may cause feelings of gender dysphoria in some people.

You may have mood swings during your pregnancy, such as suddenly feeling very irritable ortearful. Mood swings are common in most pregnancies.

Read more advice about mental health in pregnancy.

Important: You are not alone

Support is available. If you need someone to talk to now contact your maternity unit or find out where to get urgent help for mental health.

Testosterone therapy and chestfeeding

The previous use of testosterone therapy can affect milk supply in some trans men so you may not be able to chestfeed your baby.

If you can chestfeed and you also decide to start taking testosterone after having your baby, then your milk will have small amounts of testosterone in it.

It is currently unclear what, if any, risk this could pose to your baby.

You may want to discuss the pros and cons of chestfeeding and resuming testosterone therapywith your midwife or doctor.

It is also important to consider your own wellbeing if you are finding that not taking testosterone is triggering dysphoria.

Read more information on chestfeeding for trans parents.
